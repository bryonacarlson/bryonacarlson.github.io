Infrastructure Outage
Data Center Power Cascade
Category: Infrastructure / Power / Environmental

What Happened: A routine utility street power failure triggered a generator spinup. However, due to incorrectly wired UPS units (some plugged into themselves), there was no bridge power during the transition window, causing a massive device outage. When power restored, AC units failed to initialize, causing recovered devices to overheat and drop again, cascading application failures across multiple data centers via cross-system dependencies.

Root Cause: Latent infrastructure debt. Incorrect UPS wiring existed undetected until a routine utility outage exposed the vulnerability catastrophically. The generator functioned correctly; supporting gap infrastructure failed.

Key Lesson: A single point of latent failure can turn a survivable event into a multi-system disaster. Regular infrastructure audits—especially of power path wiring—are mandatory in critical data center environments.

The Friday Night P3 That Wasn't
Category: Application / Process / Escalation Culture

What Happened: At 5:30 PM on a Friday, a white-glove customer reported recurring failing transactions. A P3 bridge was opened, but the entire escalation chain (L1 up to Supervisor's Supervisor) failed to respond. Management pressure forced a priority inflation escalation to a P2 to compel engagement. A VP intervened to staff the bridge, where the supervisor challenged the designation based on policy. Upper management held the line: "If I page for a P5, you treat it like a P1 and join with urgency."

The Reality & Resolution: Once teams engaged, the issue was identified as a non-acute bug causing 1–3 failures out of 30,000 transactions every few days. The bridge disbanded by 8:30 PM, and the software defect was resolved via a standard patch cycle tracked in Jira.

Root Cause & Key Lesson: An on-call culture using ITIL priority classifications as a shield against accountability rather than a guide for response. Incident priority must reflect actual business impact and urgency—not serve as a gating mechanism for team engagement.

Operational Accountability
Weekend Coverage Gaps
Category: Staffing / On-Call Culture

What Happened: During an off-call weekend out to lunch with family, a critical incident escalated, and the scheduled on-call Incident Manager failed to respond to pages. Voluntarily stepped in, managing the crisis remotely for two hours while simultaneously paging the on-call resources and the team manager until full resolution was achieved.

Operational Context: This highlighted a recurring understaffing risk during the early stages of building out the incident management practice. Consistent willingness to cover these critical operational exposure gaps directly drove promotion tracks.

Key Lesson: "The best ability is availability." In a 24/7 operations environment, the difference between a minor outage and a major one often comes down to who picks up the phone.

Practice Maturation
Building a 24/7 Team from Scratch
Category: Organizational / Team Building / ITIL Maturity

The Inherited Situation: Took over a declining incident management team that dropped from 12 down to roughly 7 resources. Ultimately, a 3-person team absorbed the full 24/7 function, inheriting an unstable, non-ITIL-aligned practice with significant process debt and rampant priority inflation. Routine 12+ hour days were required to manage simultaneous high-priority incidents and cover shift overflows.

The Structural Solution: Engineered a global expansion strategy to achieve a mature, sustainable "Follow-the-Sun" framework. Incorporated and evaluated various regional support models, ultimately scaling and shifting the consolidated offshore hubs into a highly effective, optimized US + Costa Rica operation.

Evolution Framework: Progressed intentionally from Phase 1 (Inherited instability & reactive firefighting) through Phase 2 (ITIL-adjacent operational stabilization) to Phase 3 (True ITIL-following global framework maturity). Shifting reliance away from individual heroics and into hard process structures.
